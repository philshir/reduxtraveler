The Park

The Buffalo River, located in Northern Arkansas, was the first National River to be designated in the United States. The Buffalo River is 153 miles long. The lower 135 miles flow within the boundaries of an area. The river originates in the highest part of the Boston Mountains of the Ozarks, and finally crosses a portion of the Salem Plateau just before joining the White River. The Park is home to the state's only elk herd.

The Buffalo National River is a popular camping, hiking, canoeing, and fishing destination. Visitors may bring their own canoes or rent from several independent concessioners. The Buffalo River flows freely for 151 miles. Typically, the float season begins on the upper Buffalo in the spring. More water makes this section attractive for visitors seeking a higher level of challenge with whitewater kayaking. The season moves downstream with the months, varying from month to month with rainfall. Unlike rivers that are fed by perennial springs, the Buffalo is largely rainfall dependent. In dry years, the floating season is short and quickly moves down into the Middle and Lower Districts. In wet years, use of the upper river is prolonged with floating opportunities spread out through the park for the entire season.
