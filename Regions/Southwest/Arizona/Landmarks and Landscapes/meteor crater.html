Meteor Crater National Landmark

Southwest > Arizona > Meteor Crater

Highlights:

One of the best preserved meteorite impact craters on earth. The impact crater is one mile wide and over 500 feet deep. Located in Northwest Arizona 46 minutes away from Flaggstaff off I-40.

How to Get There

Located 43 miles from Flagstaff, AZ, halfway between Winslow and Winona. Lies 6 miles south of I-40. From Flagstaff take I-40 E. Take exit 233. Turn right onto Meteor Crater Rd for 8 min (6 mi).

Visitor Center

The visitor center is located on the rim of the Crater. The interior of the Meteor Crater is first glimpsed from within the visitor center, which has several observation areas equipped with telescopes to study the distant rock walls in more detail. The fully air-conditioned building features an 80 seat Widescreen theater, showing "Impact, the Mystery of Meteor Crater", which is included with the price of admission, indoor crater viewing area, Crater Trail access, interactive discovery center, artifacts and exhibits, including a large meteorite fragment that can be touched, an actual Apollo test capsule, gift & mineral shop and the Blasted Bistro restaurant. Also includes new Collision! 4D Experience Room.

The Crater

Outside, other viewing areas are connected by short walkways and steps that provide multiple great viewpoints of the crater. Though there is only one trail, 1/3 mile around the rim, and all hiking must be done as part of a guided tour, included with admission, along a short path to a second viewpoint a few hundred yards west of the visitor center. Rim tours are sometimes canceled during winter months when path is iced over. There is currently no hikes or access to the bottom of the crater. Some have observed the meteor crater seems smaller than volcanic created craters, such as other less well known craters like Death Valley's Ubehebe Crater. It has similar distorted strata around the sides, and the colors of the rocks are rather faded and muted; generally reddish-brown, though lighter brown around the top of the rim, which is strewn with large limestone boulders thrown up by the impact. The crater has been privately owned by the same family since early investigations began in 1902 by Daniel M. Barringer and is not affiliated with the National Park system or Arizona state parks.
