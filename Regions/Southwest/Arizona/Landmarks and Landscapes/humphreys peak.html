Humphreys Peak

Southwest > Arizona > Humphreys Peak

Highlights:

At 12,633 ft, towering over the city of Flagstaff, Humphreys peak is the highest point in Arizona . Summit walk-up trail crosses a floral meadow, up through extensive pine woods, then along a narrow, rocky ridge above the treeline.

How to Get There

How to Get There: From Flagstaff, AZ (14 mi), Take US-180 W/N Fort Valley Rd to N Snow Bowl Rd in Fort Valley. Follow N Snow Bowl Rd to trailhead access at the end of the paved road to the Arizona Snowbowl.

The Mountain

Many people that don't know anything about Arizona have no clue that there are 12,000-foot mountains in the state. The popular misconception is that Arizona is a flat desert with no trees or mountains and certainly not worth visiting. The truth is Arizona has multiple high elevation peaks populated with extensive pine woods. The highest of these is Humphreys Peak, which at 12,633 ft is the highest point in Arizona. Humphreys Peak was named after General A. A. Humphreys who was a US chief of Engineers. The trail to the summit is popular in the summer for visitors and seeking to reach one of the easier state highpoints of the Southwest compared to neighboring states in the region.

The trailhead is located the end of the paved road to the Arizona Snowbowl, a popular skiing facility usually in operation from mid November to late April, the season often extended by use of artificial snow. The 4.8 mile trail is generally wide and easy to follow, and although it has a significant elevation gain, 3,360 feet, much of the hike is not particularly steep, ascending via long, comparatively gentle switchbacks, though one section, up to a saddle linking Humphreys Peak with Agassiz Peak to the south, is rather more strenuous. The hike is made a little more difficult in some places by lengthy sections of loose stones and exposed roots but nevertheless on fair weather days the 9.6 mile round trip can be done not much more than four hours. The peak is covered in snow for most of the year except May to early October.

Trailhead

The hike starts 14 miles from downtown Flagstaff at a large parking lot just before the end of the Snowbowl Road, a paved, 6 mile route off US 180, climbing 2,000 feet. The Humphreys Peak Trail heads north across the meadow. The Humphreys Peak Trail leads through the quarter mile-wide meadow, then enters the ponderosa pine forest, where the trees are fairly well separated and the ground moderately overgrown. The wide, sometimes dusty path begins the first of four pairs of long switchbacks, over a little ravine on two occasions. The fifth switchback emerges from the woodland at the edge of a large scree slope before cutting back into the trees, and all the path climbs steadily, crossing the drainage several more times as the trees gradually become slightly more widely spaced, with a few small meadows in clearings.

The last switchback section has a tight turn from north to south after which the path straightens and moves more directly up the slope, where smaller trees allow for better views. Several much shorter switchbacks take the trail to the saddle, on a narrow ridge at 11,780 feet, from where the Humphreys summit is one mile north.

The final one mile of the Humphreys Peak Trail climbs a steepish slope covered by a patch of stunted pine trees, passes just below one intermediate peak and continues to follow the ridgeline, up to another pair of minor highpoints before the last, not-so-steep climb into the actual summit. The summit is marked by a wooden notice and a rough stone circle, from where a minor path continues a little way down a ridge to the north, though does not link with any official routes below. Hikers are rewarded with a spectacular 360 panoramic view of the surronding mountains and bragging rights to reaching the highest point in Arizona!
