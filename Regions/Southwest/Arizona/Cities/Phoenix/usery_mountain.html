Usery Mountain Regional Park

Southwest > Arizona > Phoenix > Usery Mountain Regional Park

Highlights:

Small park with good facilities, containing several square miles of Sonoran Desert flats and hills, crossed by many trails, two of which continue into the higher ground of Pass Mountain to the east; most popular is the Wind Cave Trail.

How to Get There

From Phoenix (34 mi) Get on I-10 E. Take AZ-202Loop E to E McKellips Rd in Mesa. Take exit 24 from AZ-202 S. Follow E McKellips Rd, N Ellsworth Rd/N Usery Pass Rd and E Usery Park Rd to Wind Cave Dr.

The Park

Usery Mountain Regional Park, one of 13 similar preserves managed by Maricopa County in and around Phoenix, contains 3,648 acres of Sonoran Desert flats and small rocky hills, in between the main peaks of the Usery Mountains to the west and the more extensive, and higher, summits of the Goldfield Mountains to the east. Compared to the others, the park is intermediate in character and popularity - quieter than, for example, the large South Mountain Park but busier and less rugged than White Tank Mountain, but like all it is primarily visited by locals, often for exercise and dog walking, rather than tourists, yet the scenery is very impressive, and includes all the main types of desert vegetation The most famous path is the Wind Cave Trail, which soon exits the park and climbs to a sheltered alcove high on the slopes of Pass Mountain (the westernmost peak of the Goldfield Mountains).

Wind Cave Trail/Pass Mountain Ridgeline

The trailhead is on the northern point of Wind Cave Dr. Both the Pass Mountain trail and Wind Cave trail start at the same place. You want to take the Wind Cave Trail. Hike up the popular Wind Cave Trail. The distance is 1.5 miles. Once at Wind Cave, pass the crowds and hike north on a hikers trail. A sign warns hikers to continue (only) at their own risk, along the unmaintained path to the eastern summit of the mountain. The trail heads straight up to the ridgline. Once you have made it to the ridgline the crowds are all behind you. Head north on the ridgeline. The point to the south is not the peak. Soon you will encounter some jags that must be scrambed over. This section takes a little thought and is fun scrambling over the rocks. After this point the ridgeline broadens and is much easier to cross over. Just follow the ridgeline till you get to the peak. There is one major false peak you have to hike over. The Ridgline is aproxamatly 1 mile in length. Remember to watch where your going at all times, there are cacti everywhere. The summit affords a fantastic 360 degree view of the surrounding mountains and valleys.
