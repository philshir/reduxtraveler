Wupatki National Monument

Southwest > Arizona > Wupatki NM

Highlights:

Anasazi and Sinagua ruins, built on mesa tops or the rims of shallow canyons, surrounded by the wide open plains of the Painted Desert. All have a rich red color, and were constructed from the local Moenkopi sandstone.

How to Get There

From Flagstaff (44 mi) Head east toward S San Francisco St. Turn left onto S San Francisco St. Turn right at the 1st cross street onto US-180 E/W Historic Rte 66. Continue to follow US-180 E. Continue onto US-89 N. Turn right onto Loop Rd. Turn right.
From Sunset Crater National Monument (15 mi) Head east on Fire Rd 545/Loop Rd toward Forest Service Rd 776.

Monument

Wupatki National Monument is one of several sites preserving pueblos (villages) of ancient peoples, but unlike the Tonto, Montezuma, Casa Grande Ruins and Tuzigoot monuments which contain only one main building, here there are many ruins scattered over a large area of desert northeast of Flagstaff. The pueblos all have a distinctive deep red color and were made from thin, flat blocks of the local Moenkopi sandstone. Wupatki is reached by the same loop road that passes Sunset Crater Volcano National Monument, adjoining the main north-south route US 89

History

In total there are more than 800 identified ruins spread around many miles of desert within Wupatki National Monument, but five of the largest (Wupatki, Wukoki, Lomaki, Citadel and Nalakihu) are close to the main road (see map), and these are the only sites open to visitors. All the dwellings were built by the Anasazi and Sinagua Indians during the 12th and 13th centuries This is the 3-storey Wupatki Pueblo (Hopi for 'big house') that was once home to 300 people and had over a hundred rooms. Today, travelers can hike through the park's red rocks, pausing at the village ruins along the way and learning about the day-to-day lives of the people who once called Wupatki home
